What is Resting Metabolic Rate (RMR)?

Your Resting Metabolic Rate (RMR) is an estimate of how many calories your body burns while in a state of complete rest. It represents the minimum amount of energy needed to keep your body functioning, including breathing, circulating blood, and basic brain functions. Knowing your RMR is the first and most important step in determining your total daily calorie needs, whether your goal is weight loss, gain, or maintenance.

RMR vs. BMR: What's the Difference?

You may also hear the term Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR). RMR and BMR are very similar but have a slight difference. BMR is a more restrictive measurement taken in a lab setting after an overnight fast and a full night of sleep. RMR is a more practical estimate of your resting energy expenditure and is typically about 10% higher than BMR. For most practical purposes, RMR is the more useful number for creating a diet and exercise plan.

How to Use Your RMR Result

Your RMR is your baseline. To find your Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE), you multiply your RMR by an activity factor. This gives you the total number of calories you burn in a day. The calculator provides these estimates for you, making it easy to set a calorie target for your goals.

Which formula does this calculator use?

This calculator uses the Mifflin-St Jeor equation, which is widely considered one of the most accurate methods for estimating Resting Metabolic Rate. It has been shown to be more reliable than the older Harris-Benedict equation.

How can I increase my metabolism?

While factors like age and genetics are fixed, you can boost your metabolism. The most effective way is through strength training to build more muscle mass. Muscle is more metabolically active than fat, meaning it burns more calories at rest. Eating enough protein and staying hydrated also support a healthy metabolism.

Is this RMR calculation 100% accurate?

No formula can be 100% accurate for every individual. This calculator provides a very strong scientific estimate. The most precise way to measure RMR is through a clinical test called indirect calorimetry, but this is expensive and not easily accessible. For practical purposes, this formula is an excellent starting point.
