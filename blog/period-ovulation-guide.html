The Four Phases of the Menstrual Cycle

A typical menstrual cycle lasts between 21 and 35 days, with the average being 28 days. It is governed by hormonal fluctuations and can be divided into four main phases:

  1. Menstrual Phase (The Period): This is day 1 of your cycle. The uterine lining (endometrium) is shed, resulting in bleeding that typically lasts 3 to 7 days.
  2. Follicular Phase: This phase starts on day 1 and overlaps with your period, continuing until ovulation. The pituitary gland releases Follicle-Stimulating Hormone (FSH), which prompts the ovaries to prepare an egg for release.
  3. Ovulation Phase: A surge in Luteinizing Hormone (LH) triggers the ovary to release a mature egg. This is the peak of your fertility. The egg is viable for about 12-24 hours after release.
  4. Luteal Phase: After ovulation, the follicle that released the egg transforms into the corpus luteum, which produces progesterone. This hormone thickens the uterine lining to prepare for a potential pregnancy. If fertilization doesn't occur, hormone levels drop, and the cycle begins again with menstruation.

Identifying Your Fertile Window

Your "fertile window" is the time in your cycle when you are most likely to get pregnant. It includes the five days leading up to ovulation and the day of ovulation itself. Sperm can survive in the female reproductive tract for up to five days, so intercourse during this window gives the highest chance of conception.

Beyond Calculators: Physical Signs of Ovulation

While calculators are a fantastic starting point, you can confirm your fertile window by tracking physical signs:

  • Cervical Mucus: As you approach ovulation, your cervical mucus will change from being scant and sticky to clear, slippery, and stretchy, like raw egg whites. This is the most fertile type of mucus.
  • Basal Body Temperature (BBT): Your resting body temperature will rise slightly (about 0.5¬∞F or 0.3¬∞C) after ovulation has occurred and will stay elevated until your next period.
  • Ovulation Pain (Mittelschmerz): Some women feel a slight twinge or cramp on one side of their lower abdomen when the egg is released.
